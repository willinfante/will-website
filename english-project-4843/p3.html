Casablanca (1942)
Taking place and being filmed during the Second World War, this film captures the life of Rick Blaine, a cynical refugee from France, who owns a cafe in Casablanca, Morroco. He is very neutral about the war and has not interest in being apart of the resistance. He is famous for saying, 'I stick my neck out for nobody'. Shortly into the movie he reunites with his lover (who is married to a member of the resistance) whom abandoned him in Paris, and the reason for his cynicality. Rick soon has to decide whether to escape with her to America, or to let her go with her husband who by leaving will help the Allies win the war. Ultimately, as we will see, he does the right thing for society and lets go of his selfishness.

Lessons on Individualism
Casablanca teaches us to not be individualistic. Early on in the movie, Rick frequently says, "I stick my neck out for nobody". When questioned by the Germans, he remains neutral towards the war, not caring who won. However, this changes quickly at the end of the movie. Rather than escape with Isla (which would have been selfish of him), he decides to help her and her husband escape. In doing so he ends up fighting for the Allies, since her husband is a key member of the Resistance. Ultimatley, the movie teaches us the value of collectivism towards a common good. Also at the end, we hear Rick say, "... I think this is the start of a beautiful friendship" to Louie. This also teaches us the value of social connections and shows us that even through things didn't work out as Rick had planned, he's still ok.
It's a Wonderful Life (1942)

The film begins by listening to different peoples prayers for George from around Bedford Falls. In response to all the concerned prayers, a conversation between God, Joseph, and George's Guardian Angel, Clarence ensues. God tells Clarence that George will be seriously considering throwing away His greatest gift (life). To help Clarence understand why George is in This condition, he shows him a 'rewind' of George's life since he was a little boy. George begins life with high aspirations of going out exploring and building great things. However, as he gets older this catches up to him when he realizes he has responsibilities in Bedford falls. After his father passes away, he takes over his position as President of the Bailey Building and Loan instead of going off to college. Despite also being married, he becomes discontent with the lack of opulence in his life. This crecendos when his uncle (who works at the bank with him) loses all of the banks cash. This is when God's replay ends and Clarence is sent down to save George. Clarence teaches George the value of his life by showing him what the world would have been life without him (which would be a disaster). George then returns to his life happy, knowing that he has lots of friends and has had a positive impact on all of them. All along George learns that he has been focused on the wrong things.

Decreased importance of religion in Americans lives

Reflected in frequent religious references...

→ Casablanca: Isla says 'God bless you' to Rick multiple times in the movie (not as prominent as in the other movie but nevertheless more present than in new movies).

→ It's a Wonderful Life is full of religous references: the whole movie is God showing Clarence (George's Guardian Angel) George's whole life so that he could help him.


• Decreased role in religion could be contributing to the mental health crisis through the channels of the values religions portray. For example, religion goes against materialism:

'...materialism tends to be associated with treating others in more competitive, manipulative and selfish ways, as well as with being less empathetic ... [M]aterialism is associated with lower levels of well-being, less pro-social interpersonal behavior, more ecologically destructive behavior, and worse academic outcomes. It also is associated with more spending problems and debt... ...We found that the more highly people endorsed materialistic values, the more they experienced unpleasant emotions, depression and anxiety, the more they reported physical health problems, such as stomachaches and headaches, and the less they experienced pleasant emotions and felt satisfied with their lives.' (Kasser 2014)

Closing thoughts....

Media has changed over the past 100 years and with it values of America have changed. Some of these values include, but are not limited to: social connections, materialism, perfectionism, and general positivity. It seems that these changing values (less emphasis on others, more materialism, more perfectionism, and less general positivity) as reflected in the media today have perpetuated the mental health crisis that we see in America today. If people could pay more attention to what their values are and what they believe in, perhaps the crisis would get better compared to where we are today.
